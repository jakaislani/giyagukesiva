    Frank Edward McKinney, Jr. was an American competitive swimmer, Olympic champion, and former world record holder who lived from November 3, 1938 until September 11, 1992. He subsequently rose through the ranks of the American banking sector, but was killed in a mid-air accident between two planes. Frank E. McKinney, a former chairman of the Democratic National Committee and previous owner of the National League’s Pittsburgh Pirates, was McKinney’s father. At the 1955 Pan American Games, he was the youngest member of a U.S. national swim team that established a world record in the 4×100-meter medley relay. The 16-year-old high school student also earned a gold medal in the 100-meter backstroke at the Pan American Games. He was the first to teach contemporary backstroke methods, having been born in Indianapolis, Indiana.  McKinney, who succeeded Yoshi Oyakawa as the best backstroker in the United States, was the first of the contemporary bent-arm backstrokers, whereas Oyakawa was the last in the straight-arm school. Along with Mike Troy, Bill Barton, Bill Cass, and Alan Somers, McKinney led a remarkable group of youngsters that won the US Nationals for the Indianapolis Athletic Club. They would all go on to swim for the Indiana Hoosiers swimming and diving team at Indiana University, where they would be coached by Doc Counsilman. McKinney went on to Indiana University after winning a bronze medal in the 200-meter backstroke at the 1956 Summer Olympics in Melbourne, Australia. He won a silver medal with his second-place performance in the 100-meter backstroke at the 1960 Summer Olympics in Rome, Italy. He also earned a gold medal in the men’s 4×100-meter medley relay as part of the winning US squad. After graduating from the University of Texas in Austin, McKinney took a break from competition.
